Kickoff Example scenario:
Dry & wet Anthrax production
“Wet anthrax takes the form of a liquid slurry.  Dry anthrax is a non-liquid, powder-like substance.  The production methods used to develop wet and dry anthrax share some common elements, but differ in several key final process steps.  Fermentation is used to grow the anthrax spores, regardless of the wet or dry end state.  Following fermenation, however, dry agents then go through several process steps  including milling and drying (e.g., spray dry, vacuum drum dry, or azeotropic distillation) - that the wet agent does not.  Once produced, dry agents have far less stringent storage requirements than wet agent, which require environmentally-controlled storage.  Also, dry agent can be dispensed more easily than wet agent, which requires sophisticated aerosol dispersion mechanisms to acheive the most effective spore size.
Anthrax in dry form, is far more difficult to produce - in terms of product stabilization and quality (i.e., spore size), and in terms of worker safety.  On the other hand, dry agents are very easy to dispense and are hardier in the environment - hence their battlefield utility.  Liquid agent dispensers require very precise aerosol mechanisms to yield the desired particle size without too much product destruction in the process.  The principal tradeoff between production difficulty and delivery difficulty is one which occurs with many BW agents.  This takes us then to the particular actor involved, and what they're expected objective and delivery mode is likely to be.  An Iraqi program designed to produce an agent for battlefield employment, with the resources of a state actor, is more likely to pursue the dry agent form.  A terrorist actor, with more limited resources and different employment objectives, might instead pursue the more simple production avenue of the liquid agent, even sacrificing dispensing efficiency.”
                                                                                                                [PSR, April 2000]

EKCP Analysis Part 2:
Problem Solving Methods for
Evaluation and Tradeoff Assessment

Tradeoff Analysis requires Representing and Comparing Alternatives
Criteria: cost, duration, easiness-to-dispense, hardiness-in-envmt,…
Actor’s objectives lead to particular preferences on some criteria
Objective = Battlefield employment
à easiness-to-dispense [high better]
à hardiness-in-environment [high better]
Prioritized in case of conflict
Example: hardiness-in-environment is more important than easiness-to-dispense
Actor’s resources and capabilities result in additional constraints
Example: wet anthrax requires sophisticated aerosol dispersion mechanisms

Representing Evaluation Criteria
Criteria are ultimately ground on detailed knowledge about the BW process models
Hardiness-in-environment: derived from spore size, whether it is on liquid slurry, env. conditions such as temperature, etc.
Easiness-to-dispense: derived from spray method used, spore size at time of dispersal, whether powder or liquid, etc.
Production-feasibility: derived from what equipment is needed & its availability, etc.

Approach: Use PSMs for Evaluation and Tradeoff Assessment
Problem Solving Method (PSM) captures domain-independent principles/core theories about processes
Based on ISI’s work on plan evaluation PSMs [Blythe and Gil 2000]
PSM is extended with domain-specific knowledge for BW assessment criteria

Three Stages in Tradeoff Assessment
1) Abstraction: deriving abstract features of a process based on primitive features
2) Preference Generation: preference/selection/rejection knowledge is applied to the abstracted features
3) Tradeoff analysis: preferences are compared and prioritized

Acquiring BW Process Models
Three stages :
I) user specifies the steps in the process model
What steps are substeps of others, alternative substeps, ordering
e.g.: Producing dry Anthrax has 3 ordered substeps: ferment, dry and mill
Can use PSR’s CIM tool, Cmaps, commercial process tools, etc.
II) user specifies for each step: personnel, material, equipment, duration
e.g., materials include wet rice
III) user models specific steps in detail
Draws from the library components relevant to speficic steps
e.g., what does fermentation do to Anthrax spores
User links the materials & equipment specified in stage II to specific roles in the components
e.g., wet rice is mapped to “growth medium” in the fermentation component
